State of matter test

A state of matter is one of the four forms that any substance in nature can take: solid, liquid, gas or plasma. Depending on its state, matter will have different properties (density, refractive index, crystal structure, etc… ). These properties will be linked to specific behaviors (viscosity, malleability, etc… ).



Solid


A solid has its own volume (very difficult to compress) and its own shape. However, it can deform under the effect of stresses depending on its ductility and elasticity. When they are solid, the particles (atoms, ions or molecules) bind together by chemical bonds allowing their stabilization.




Liquid


A liquid also has its own volume. However, it does not have its own shape because it will take the shape of the container that will contain it. It will always have a flat, horizontal surface (in a uniform gravity field). Liquid particles are only weakly bound: they can move relative to each other, but are not independent of each other. It is a fluid state which is totally deformable.




Gaseous


A gas has neither its own volume nor its own form. Indeed, it will try to occupy all the volume at its disposal.The gaseous particles are very weakly linked: they are said to be almost independent. It is also a fluid state and therefore deformable. A body in the gaseous state consists only of atoms and molecules.




Plasma


It has neither its own volume, nor its own form, nor even of measurable stable energy. However, it has a very different fluidic form from the other 3 states. It is also rarer than the other 3 forms, due to its very high temperatures.It is characterized as a highly ionized gas. Plasma particles are detached from their electrons

A state of matter is also characterized by phase transitions. This transition indicates a change in structure and sometimes also in properties. The condition of an assembly may vary depending on the pressure and temperature conditions. For example, the solid to liquid transition occurs by increasing the temperature.


In a chemical equation, the solid state is represented by (s), the liquid state by (l), gas by (g), and an aqueous solution by (aq). The plasma state is rarely used, but, if present, is represented by (p).



Other conditions of matter exist but they are quite rare because they are only achievable for certain matter under certain conditions. Here is the list:


  • quasi-crystal
  • supercritical state
  • superconducting state at very low temperature
  • mesomorphic state of liquid crystals (intermediate between liquid and solid)
  • Bose-Einstein condensate (superfluid or rubidium condensate for example)
  • ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic state
  • supersolid state
  • Jahn-Teller metals (both insulating and superconducting)
  • disordered hyper-uniform state (crystalline and liquid state)
